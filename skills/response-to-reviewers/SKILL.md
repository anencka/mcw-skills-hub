# Response to Reviewers

## When to Use
A manuscript came back with reviews (major/minor revision), or a grant is being resubmitted
and needs its Introduction responding to the summary statement.

## First-use setup (read the profile; fill if thin)
Read `~/work-profile/communication-style.md` (how deferential vs. firm the operator wants to
be) and `~/work-profile/current-projects.md`. Gather the artifacts for this task: the decision
letter / summary statement, the reviews verbatim, the original submission, and what has
actually changed since (new data, new analyses).

## Procedure
1. **Decompose the reviews** into atomic, numbered points — one row per distinct criticism,
   preserving each reviewer's numbering and quoting them verbatim. Nothing merged away:
   dodged points are the #1 cause of angry re-reviews.
2. **Triage each point**: agree-and-fix / partially agree / respectfully rebut. The operator
   decides the rebuttals — propose a stance, don't assume one, especially where the fix
   would require new experiments.
3. **Draft responses** in the standard register: thank → state what was done → point to the
   exact change ("Results, p. 8, lines 143-150; new Figure 3"). For rebuttals: concede
   what's fair, then the evidence-based case — cite data or literature (verify via
   `literature-research`; never invent a supporting citation).
4. **Consistency pass:** every claimed change exists in the revised document; reviewers
   compare. Contradictory reviewers get individually respectful answers plus a shared
   explanation of the choice made.
5. **Grant resubmissions:** condense into the one-page Introduction — summary of criticisms
   (grouped, not itemized), responses, and what's new; mark substantial changes as the
   funder requires. Same no-dodge rule at page scale.
6. **Deliver** the response letter + a change summary table (point — response type —
   location of change), formatted per journal/funder convention.

## Pitfalls
- Dodging or half-answering a point and hoping the reviewer won't notice. They notice.
- Claiming a change that wasn't made, or "we have clarified" with no location cited.
- Sarcasm or wounded tone in rebuttals — firm and evidence-based, never defensive.
- Treating "the reviewer misunderstood" as the reviewer's fault: if they misread it, the
  text was unclear — fix the text too.
- Letting the response letter drift out of sync with late edits to the manuscript.

## Verification
Every review point appears verbatim with a response and a change location (or an explicit,
evidence-backed rebuttal the operator approved); every cited change is really in the revised
document; tone is professional throughout; page/format limits met for resubmission intros.
